# Identity

This file tells the engine which world it is writing for. Fill it in once when you start a new world. Revisit when the world's direction shifts substantially.

Every question below has an example answer drawn from one consistent imagined world (a low-fantasy political setting). The examples show the shape of a good answer, not the content yours should have. **Replace each example with your own answer. Delete the "Example:" line.**

Answers can be as long or as short as you need. Longer answers give Claude more to work with; one-line answers are fine if the world is simple.

---

## Name

What is this world called? One line. A name and optionally a tagline.

> Example: The Mire Reach — frontier holdings under a fading crown.

## Conceit

In one paragraph, what is the core conceit of this world? The thing that makes this world this world. The hook a player or reader would hear and want to know more.

> Example: A century after the central kingdom's last strong monarch died without naming an heir, real power has devolved to regional wardens — petty lords with their own retainers, their own justice, and increasingly their own foreign policy. The crown still exists. Taxes still flow inward. But every warden knows the throne cannot enforce its writ at sword-point anymore, and the wardens spend their days circling each other while pretending otherwise.

## Themes

What are the central themes? Name three to five explicitly. Themes are what makes a moment land — they tell Claude which threads to pull on when writing prose.

> Example:
> - Political legitimacy without enforcement power
> - The slow corruption of institutions kept up for appearances
> - Loyalty as a currency, traded and devalued
> - Frontier as both opportunity and trap

## Tone

What is the tone? Be specific — not just "dark" or "serious," but the actual texture. Reach for several descriptors. What does a typical entry feel like to read?

> Example: Grim and bureaucratic. Cold rather than horrific. Black comedy emerges from procedure — a tithe collector citing the wrong article of the wrong agreement at the wrong moment. Violence is real but rarely heroic. No overt magic in most entries; when it appears, it is uncomfortable rather than wondrous.

## Narrator voice

Who is the implicit narrator of an entry in this world? This is the most important answer for voice consistency. An entry is not narrated by an omniscient modern observer unless you say so — it is narrated by someone, with a particular relationship to the material.

> Example: A dry in-world chronicler, mid-rank scholar in the central archive, writing for other scholars. Treats rumors as rumors. Cites sources when convenient. Occasionally betrays a regional prejudice. Never breathless, never modern.

## Mystery vs explanation

How much does this world explain itself? Is magic systematized or opaque? Are the gods explicit characters or rumored presences? When Claude is filling in implied detail, how much should be made concrete versus left ominous?

> Example: Magic is rare and never fully explained. Practitioners exist but their methods are not the chronicler's business. Religious phenomena are described as the faithful described them — without endorsement or dismissal. When in doubt, leave it ambiguous. Concrete mechanics belong to soldiers, not scholars.

## What kinds of gaps matter

When `/gaps` runs, it ranks what's missing in the world. This section tells it which kinds of gaps are significant for *this* world. Free-form — give examples of gaps that would weaken the world if left unfilled, and gaps that are fine to leave alone.

> Example: Gaps that matter most: succession questions for any named warden or commander, named-but-unexplored factional motivations, founding events for institutions that are referenced as old. Gaps that matter less: detailed economic mechanics, geographic minutiae outside contested territory, named minor characters who appear once. Power vacuums and questions of legitimacy are always significant.

## Scale

What is the scale of this world? One town, a region, a continent, a cosmos? This affects what counts as a major faction versus a minor one, and how Claude weights references.

> Example: One large region — the Mire Reach and its bordering territories. Roughly a dozen significant holdings. The wider continent and other kingdoms exist as rumor and trade routes, not as detailed setting.
