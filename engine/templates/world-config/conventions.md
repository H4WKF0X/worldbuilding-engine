# Conventions

This file tells the engine how the writing of this world works. Naming, dates, titles, cross-references, tense, and any in-world terms that need special handling.

Like `identity.md`, each section has an example answer from one consistent imagined world. **Replace each example with your own answer. Delete the "Example:" line.**

Sections you don't have strong feelings about can be answered briefly — the engine's defaults will handle anything you don't override.

---

## Naming conventions

How are names structured in this world? Per culture, per region, per entity type — whatever matters. The goal is for Claude to invent plausible names when filling in implied characters and places.

> Example:
> - The central kingdom: names of Anglo-Saxon texture (Aelthorn, Mirelle, Vance, Reyne). Two syllables most common, occasionally three.
> - The Stormlands (eastern reach): doubled consonants and harder edges (Korr, Hessil, Tannath).
> - Places: descriptors plus geographic features (Greybridge, Long Mire, Stonewake). Avoid generic fantasy compounds (no Ironforge, no Shadowvale).
> - Religious figures: titled by office, not by name in first reference — "Brother Vance" not "Vance."

## Dates and timeline

How are dates referenced in entries? In-world calendar with explicit years? Relative ("three decades past")? Reign-based ("in the eighth year of King X")?

> Example: In-world year numbers when known, written as "year 412" or "412 IR" (Imperial Reckoning). Relative phrasing for vaguer events — "three winters past," "in the chronicler's grandfather's time." Avoid out-of-world calendar references.

## Titles and honorifics

How do titles work? When are full titles used versus shortened forms?

> Example: Full title on first reference within an entry — "Lord Aelthorn," "Stormwarden Mirelle," "Brother Vance." Family name alone on subsequent references for nobility ("Aelthorn"). Religious figures always retain "Brother" or "Sister." Titles for low-status figures (sergeant, factor) are lowercased and used naturally without ceremony.

## Cross-reference style

When Claude is writing an entry and mentions another entity, how should that read? The wikilink itself is required (engine rule), but the prose around it has style.

> Example: First mention of an entity in an entry: `[[Lord Aelthorn]]`. Subsequent mentions in the same entry: shortened name without wikilink ("Aelthorn rode out at dawn"). Place names follow the same rule. Factions are treated as collective singulars — "the Stormwardens hold the eastern reach" not "the Stormwardens holds."

## POV and tense

What point of view and tense do entries use?

> Example: Third person past tense throughout. The chronicler is not present in the prose — no "we," no "I." Occasionally the chronicler's perspective leaks in through hedged language ("it is said," "by some accounts"), but never as direct narration.

## In-world terms with specific meanings

Are there terms in this world that have specific in-fiction meanings Claude should preserve and not generalize?

> Example:
> - "The Compact" always refers to the Greybridge Compact specifically, never as a generic word for agreement.
> - "Riders" without qualifier means Stormwarden cavalry, not horsemen in general.
> - "The Faith" capitalized means the Quiet Faith; lowercase "faith" is generic belief.
> - "Warden" is a political title (regional governor), not a synonym for guard.

## Forbidden devices

Anything that would break the world's voice if it appeared in an entry? Be specific — broad bans ("no modern language") are less useful than specific ones ("no idioms that depend on gunpowder," "no narrator winking at the reader").

> Example:
> - No modern idioms or anachronistic metaphors (no "running on fumes," no "moving the needle").
> - No exclamation marks in narration. Dialogue may use them sparingly.
> - No metaphors involving things that don't exist in-world (no "like a clockwork mechanism" — there are no clocks).
> - The chronicler does not address the reader directly.
> - Em dashes are fine in prose, used sparingly.
